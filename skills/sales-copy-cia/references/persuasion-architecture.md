## 1. CIALDINI'S FRAMEWORK {#1-cialdinis-framework}

### The 7 Principles
1. **Reciprocity** — Uninvited gifts create obligation. Regan (1971): free soda → 2x raffle ticket purchases. Reciprocity overrides liking. Asymmetric — returned at greater value than given.
2. **Commitment/Consistency** — Public, active, effortful commitments create internal pressure. Exploits dissonance + self-perception.
3. **Social Proof** — Uncertainty → look to others. System 1 pattern matching.
4. **Authority** — Deference to expertise/power bypasses System 2.
5. **Liking** — Attractiveness (halo), similarity, compliments, cooperation, association. System 1 affective processing.
6. **Scarcity** — Rare/diminishing = valuable. Activates loss aversion + reactance simultaneously.
7. **Unity** — Shared identity > liking. "Not liking — belonging." Familial bonds override affective preference. Asking for "advice" (not "opinions") activates togetherness → significantly more positive responses.

### Pre-Suasion (2016)

**Core thesis:** The factor most likely to determine choice is often not the most accurate — it's the one elevated in attention at the moment of decision.

**Two exploited biases:**
- **Focalism:** What we focus on, we assume is important BECAUSE we're focusing on it.
- **Causality bias:** Attentional focus → perceived causality.

**Key studies:**
- **Clouds & Coins (Mandel & Johnson 2002):** Fluffy cloud background → comfort prioritized. Penny background → cost prioritized. No conscious awareness of influence.
- **Helpful Person Opener:** "Do you consider yourself helpful?" before survey request → compliance jumped 29% → 77.3% (166% increase).
- **Social Happiness Framing:** "Are you unhappy with your social life?" → 375% more likely to declare unhappiness vs. positively framed question.

**Opener strategy:** Bring focus to a concept nested in the forthcoming message BEFORE delivery. Makes it cognitively accessible and important.

## 2. DUAL-PROCESS SYSTEM {#2-dual-process}

### System 1 vs System 2 (Kahneman 2011)
| System 1 | System 2 |
|----------|----------|
| Fast, effortless, unconscious | Slow, effortful, conscious |
| Involuntary, parallel, error-prone | Voluntary, serial, can override |
| Emotion integrated | Can override when active |

**Critical insight:** System 2 is lazy. Defaults to endorsing System 1 unless triggered. Persuasion = keep System 2 disengaged OR engage it with arguments strong enough to survive scrutiny.

### Elaboration Likelihood Model (Petty & Cacioppo 1986)
- **Central route (System 2):** High motivation + ability → argument quality matters → durable change, strong behavior predictor.
- **Peripheral route (System 1):** Low motivation or ability → heuristic cues (attractiveness, argument count, social proof, authority) → temporary, fragile change.

### Keeping System 2 Asleep
- **Cognitive ease** (repetition, priming, good mood, clear presentation) → feelings of truth and safety.
- **Cognitive load** — When System 2 is taxed (complex proposals, time pressure, multitasking), System 1 runs unchecked.
- **WYSIATI** — System 1 builds best story from available info without checking what's missing. Confidence depends on story quality, NOT evidence quality.

## 3. BEHAVIORAL ECONOMICS {#3-behavioral-economics}

### Prospect Theory (Kahneman & Tversky 1979)
- **Reference dependence:** Outcomes evaluated relative to status quo, not absolute.
- **Diminishing sensitivity:** $100→$200 feels bigger than $1100→$1200.
- **Loss aversion coefficient: λ = 2.25** — losses felt ~2.25x more intensely than equivalent gains (range 2.0-2.5).

**Applications:**
- "You're losing $X/month" > "You could save $X/month"
- Trials exploit endowment effect — possession → loss aversion on return
- Gain frame → risk aversion (prefer sure $500). Loss frame → risk seeking (prefer 50% chance of -$1000 over sure -$500).

### Anchoring (Tversky & Kahneman 1974)
- **Wheel of Fortune study:** Random spin of 10 vs 65 shifted UN estimates by 20 percentage points.
- **Multiplication study:** 8x7x6... median 2,250 vs 1x2x3... median 512. (Correct: 40,320)
- Telling people about anchoring bias + monetary incentives does NOT eliminate it.
- $39 vs $40 perceived as ~$10 cheaper (left-digit anchoring).
- **Application:** First number in negotiation strongly biases all counteroffers.

### Endowment Effect (Knetsch 1989, Kahneman et al. 1991)
- Mug/chocolate study: only 1 in 10 switched after receiving an item.
- Mechanism: loss aversion. Giving up possessed item = loss > utility of acquiring replacement.

### Status Quo Bias — Three channels:
1. **Ease** — Default requires no effort
2. **Endorsement** — Default = implicit recommendation
3. **Endowment** — Default feels "owned"

### Choice Architecture (Thaler & Sunstein 2008)
- Default effects (most powerful nudge)
- Ordering effects (first/prominent chosen disproportionately)
- Decoy effects (inferior third option shifts preference between original two)
- Simplification (reduce friction on desired path, add friction to undesired)

## 4. SOCIAL PROOF {#4-social-proof}

### Two Types (Deutsch & Gerard 1955)
- **Informational:** Others' behavior = evidence about reality. Produces genuine belief change. Persists.
- **Normative:** Conforming to avoid social rejection. Public compliance without private acceptance. Reverts.

### Asch Conformity (1951, 1956)
- 43% public conformity (wrong answer to match group)
- 12.5% private conformity
- 1% control error rate
- Most conformers KNEW group was wrong but feared social cost.
- Competition between groups increased conformity further.

### Milgram Obedience (1963)
- 65% administered max 450V shock on authority's order
- 100% reached 300V
- 40% with victim in same room, 30% holding victim's arm, 21% by phone, ~10% when confederates disobeyed
- **Agentic state:** Person views self as instrument of authority, transfers responsibility.

### Hotel Towel Study (Goldstein, Cialdini, Griskevicius 2008)
- Provincial norm ("guests in THIS ROOM") > generic norm ("majority of guests") > environmental appeal
- **Key:** Localized, situationally matched social proof > generic.
- Combining descriptive (what people DO) + injunctive (what people APPROVE) = strongest effect.

## 5. COMMITMENT & CONSISTENCY {#5-commitment}

### Cognitive Dissonance (Festinger 1957, Festinger & Carlsmith 1959)
- Tedious peg-turning task. $1 group rated task MORE positively than $20 group.
- $20 = sufficient justification ("I lied for money"). $1 = insufficient justification → dissonance → attitude change ("I must have enjoyed it").
- **Implication:** Less external pressure → more internalized attitude change. Don't oversell.

### Foot-in-the-Door (Freedman & Fraser 1966)
- Small request (answer cancer questions) → large request (2-hour home inspection)
- FITD: 53% agreed. Control: 22%. **141% increase.**
- Mechanism: Initial compliance → self-perception shift ("I'm the kind of person who...") → consistent behavior.

### Kiesler's Commitment Properties (1971)
- Commitment is continuous (more or less, not binary)
- **Public > private** commitments
- **Effortful > easy** commitments
- **Freely chosen > coerced** commitments
- Preparatory acts freeze the system of possible choices.

### Self-Perception Theory (Bem 1972)
- People observe own behavior → infer attitudes ("I did X, so I must be the kind of person who values X")
- Especially when internal cues are ambiguous
- Converges with dissonance in practice: small commitments shift self-concept → drive larger behaviors.

## 6. SCARCITY & REACTANCE {#6-scarcity}

### Psychological Reactance (Brehm 1966)
- Perceived threat to freedom → aversive motivational state → drive to restore freedom
- Not mere annoyance — specific arousal state
- Restricted behavior becomes MORE attractive than before restriction

### Romeo & Juliet Effect (Driscoll et al. 1972)
- Parental interference → increased love over 6 months
- Caveat: Mixed replications. May depend on relationship type, interference nature, culture.

### Commodity Theory (Brock 1968)
- Anything possessed, useful, transferable → valued proportional to scarcity
- Scarcity → feelings of personal distinctiveness/uniqueness

### Cookie Jar Experiment (Worchel et al. 1975)
1. Scarce (2 cookies) > abundant (10 cookies) in desirability
2. Abundant→scarce > always-scarce (loss amplifies)
3. Scarce due to demand > scarce due to accident (social competition strongest)

**Triple mechanism:** Commodity theory (scarcity=value) + loss aversion (having→not-having=loss) + reactance (restriction→desire to restore)

### Scarcity Cascade in Consumer Behavior
1. Perception of scarcity/restriction
2. Reactance arousal
3. Increased desire
4. Urgency (loss aversion)
5. Reduced deliberation (System 2 bypassed)
6. Impulsive compliance

## 7. TRUST & AUTHORITY {#7-trust}

### Oxytocin (Kosfeld et al. 2005, Nature)
- Intranasal oxytocin → substantial increase in trust (money transferred to anonymous partners)
- Effect specific to social risk, not general financial risk
- Zak: Compelling narratives cause oxytocin release. High oxytocin → generosity increases up to 80%.
- **Caveats:** Replication found no effect. Oxytocin may amplify social salience generally (positive AND negative), not trust specifically.

### Authority Heuristic
- Triggers: titles, clothing/uniforms, expensive trappings, institutional affiliation
- Bypasses System 2 — authority markers as compliance shortcut

### Credibility = Expertise + Trustworthiness
- Admitting weakness INCREASES overall persuasiveness (signals honesty → transfers credibility to all claims)

### Sleeper Effect (Hovland & Weiss 1951, Pratkanis et al. 1988)
- Messages from low-credibility sources: persuasion INCREASES over time
- Source-message association weakens → discounting cue decays faster than message impact
- **Conditions:** Recipients paid attention to content, cue came AFTER message, rated source trustworthiness

## 8. EMOTIONAL DECISION MAKING {#8-emotion}

### Somatic Marker Hypothesis (Damasio 1994, Bechara et al. 1994)
- **Body loop:** Actual physiological changes (sweating, heart rate) feed back to brain
- **As-if body loop:** Brain simulates body state WITHOUT actual stimulus — faster, no event needed
- **Iowa Gambling Task:** Healthy participants showed anticipatory skin conductance BEFORE choosing risky decks — before conscious awareness. vmPFC-damaged patients couldn't use emotional signals → catastrophic decisions despite intact logic.
- **Conclusion:** Rational decision-making REQUIRES emotional processing.

### Affect Heuristic (Slovic 2002)
- Objects/events tagged with positive/negative affect
- Positive affect → risk DOWN + benefit UP simultaneously (inverse correlation that doesn't exist in reality)
- Affective responses activate within milliseconds, before conscious analysis
- Risk-as-feelings dominates risk-as-analysis in most real decisions

### Anticipated Emotions
- Anticipated regret → risk aversion, decision avoidance
- Anticipated pride/satisfaction → goal-directed behavior
- Anticipated shame → conformity and compliance
- Function as System 1 inputs biasing decisions before rational analysis

### Emotional Contagion (Hatfield, Cacioppo, Rapson 1993)
- **Three-stage:** Automatic mimicry → afferent feedback (forced smile → mild positive emotion) → emotional convergence
- People's own emotions more influenced by others' nonverbal cues than words
- Operates below conscious awareness, resistant to rational override
- **Sales:** Genuine enthusiasm is physiologically contagious. Anxiety/desperation equally contagious and repulsive.
