# Systems Thinking — Core Concepts

---

## Stocks and Flows

**Stock:** A quantity you can observe and measure at any moment. An accumulation. Water in a bathtub, money in a bank, population of a city, trust in a relationship, knowledge in an organisation.

**Flow:** A rate of change. Inflows increase the stock; outflows decrease it.

**Key principles:**
- **Stocks change slowly, even when flows change suddenly.** Systems have momentum and memory. A bathtub doesn't empty instantly. This creates lag between action and result.
- **Stocks act as buffers.** They decouple inflows from outflows. A warehouse decouples production from sales.
- **You can only change a stock by changing its flows.** No shortcuts. To reduce debt, increase payments or decrease borrowing.
- **Dynamic equilibrium ≠ nothing happening.** It means inflows = outflows. A "stable" population of 10M can have 200K births and 200K deaths per year.

**Meadows:** "If you understand the dynamics of stocks and flows, you understand a good deal of the behavior of complex systems."

## Feedback Loops

### Reinforcing (Positive)
Amplifies change. Growth breeds growth. Decline breeds decline. Source of exponential growth AND collapse.

**Structure:** More stock → More inflow → Even more stock

**Examples:** Compound interest, word of mouth, erosion of confidence, arms races, learning curves, network effects.

**Left unchecked:** Exponential growth or collapse. Nothing physical sustains exponential growth forever — a balancing loop always intervenes.

### Balancing (Negative)
Resists change. Pushes toward a goal or equilibrium. Source of stability and resistance.

**Structure:** Discrepancy between actual and goal → Corrective action → Reduced discrepancy

**Examples:** Thermostat, body temperature, inventory management, market correction, hunger.

**Critical question:** Whose goal? The system's goal may not match the stated goal of the people in it.

### Dominance Shifts
Multiple loops operate simultaneously. Behaviour depends on which is dominant. Dominance shifts as conditions change. S-curves happen when a reinforcing loop dominates early, then a balancing loop takes over.

## Delays

Pervasive in systems. One of the most common causes of oscillation, overshoot, and poor decisions.

**Types:** Information delays (time to know), response delays (time to decide), physical delays (time to happen).

**How delays cause oscillation:**
1. Discrepancy detected → corrective action taken
2. Effect not seen immediately (delay)
3. Impatient, more corrective action
4. All delayed corrections arrive at once → overshoot
5. Now too far the other way → overcorrect again

**Classic examples:** Shower temperature, bullwhip effect in supply chains, boom-bust commodity cycles, policy oscillations.

**Principle:** When there are significant delays, slow down. Act less aggressively. Monitor trends, not snapshots.

## Bounded Rationality

People make perfectly rational decisions from their limited vantage point, yet produce collectively irrational outcomes.

- Each actor sees only their part of the system
- They optimise locally, not globally
- They respond to current, local information — not delayed, distant effects
- The system behaves pathologically even though every individual is "reasonable"

**Example:** Each herder rationally adds one cow (benefit accrues to them; overgrazing cost is shared). Each is rational. The commons collapses.

**Implication:** Don't blame individuals for systemic problems. Change the structure — information flows, incentives, rules, goals — and the same people produce different behaviour.

## Emergence

Properties of a whole system not present in any individual part. Cannot be predicted from knowledge of parts alone.

**Examples:** Consciousness from neurons, traffic jams from individual driving, culture from interactions, market prices from buy/sell decisions.

**Key properties:**
- Irreducible (can't reduce to parts)
- Downward causation (the whole constrains its parts)
- Novelty (genuinely new, not just complicated combinations)
- Unpredictability (can't predict from parts, though understood after the fact)

**Practical implication:** You cannot design emergent properties directly. You create conditions from which they're likely to emerge. Manage the soil, not the plant.

---

## Non-Linearity

Output is not proportional to input. Small causes can have large effects. Large causes can have small effects.

**Types:**
- **Thresholds/tipping points:** Nothing happens until a critical level, then everything changes
- **Exponential dynamics:** Small initial advantage compounds (compound interest, viral spread)
- **Saturation:** Diminishing returns beyond a point
- **Hysteresis:** Path from A→B differs from B→A (losing trust is fast; rebuilding is slow)

**Why it matters:** Linear thinking is deeply embedded but fails in most real systems. We underestimate compounding risks and overestimate our ability to create proportional results.

---

## Resilience

The ability to survive and recover from perturbation. NOT the same as stability.

- **Stability** = ability to stay the same
- **Resilience** = ability to recover and continue (possibly in different configuration)

**Sources:** Diversity, redundancy, modularity (loose coupling), tight feedback, reserves/buffers, social capital, adaptive capacity.

**Warning:** Resilience is invisible until it's lost. Systems optimised for efficiency may have sacrificed resilience — making them brittle.

**Resilience thinking:** Systems can exist in multiple stable states (regimes). Tipping points can flip between regimes. Some flips are irreversible.

---

## Self-Organisation

A system creating new structures and behaviours without external direction. The most remarkable property of living and adaptive systems.

**Requires:** Freedom to experiment, diversity, connectivity, error tolerance, resource slack.

**Meadows:** "Systems that are most self-organizing are the most resilient. Insistence on a single right way narrows self-organizing and reduces resilience."

---

## Hierarchy

Complex systems organise into nested subsystems. Not imposed top-down — emerges because it works. Reduces information processing burden.

**Healthy:** Upper levels serve lower levels. Information flows both ways. Subsystems have autonomy within boundaries.

**Pathological:** Upper levels exploit lower levels. Information distorted flowing up. Subsystems too autonomous (fragmentation) or too controlled (rigidity).

---

## Mental Models

Internal representations of how the world works. Always simplifications. Often unconscious. Filter what we perceive.

**Double-loop learning (Argyris):** Single-loop adjusts actions. Double-loop adjusts the mental model that produces actions.

**Ladder of inference:**
1. Observable data → 2. Select data (based on model) → 3. Add meanings → 4. Make assumptions → 5. Draw conclusions → 6. Adopt beliefs → 7. Take actions → loop back to step 2

Mental models become self-reinforcing: beliefs affect what data you select.

---

## Unintended Consequences

A defining feature of complex systems. Actions have effects beyond what's intended because of interconnection, feedback, delays, non-linearity, and bounded rationality.

**Classic examples:**
- Cobra effect: bounties for dead cobras → people breed cobras
- Rent control → reduced housing supply → worse affordability
- Road widening → induced demand → same congestion
- Social media for connection → polarisation, addiction

**Principle:** In complex systems, assume there will be unintended consequences. Design for reversibility. Monitor for surprises. Stay humble.
