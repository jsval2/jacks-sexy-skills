# System Archetypes — Full Catalog

Generic system structures that produce predictable behaviour. Recognising the archetype → diagnose the structure → find the leverage point.

---

## 1. Fixes That Fail
**Structure:** Quick fix addresses symptom. Works short-term. Unintended side effects make original problem worse.

**Examples:**
- Stimulants for lack of sleep → adrenal fatigue → worse exhaustion
- Cutting prices to boost sales → margin erosion → worse sales
- Borrowing to cover cash shortfall → debt service → worse cash flow
- Firefighting instead of prevention → no time for prevention → more fires

**Leverage:** Resist the quick fix. Invest in the fundamental solution.
**Diagnostic:** "Is this addressing the symptom or the cause?"

---

## 2. Shifting the Burden (Addiction)
**Structure:** Symptomatic solution (quick, addictive) vs. fundamental solution (slow, lasting). Symptomatic fix reduces pressure for fundamental fix. Capacity atrophies. Dependency grows.

**Examples:**
- Relying on consultants instead of building internal capability
- Manager swooping in instead of developing team skills
- Medication for lifestyle diseases instead of changing lifestyle

**Leverage:** Strengthen the fundamental solution while weaning off the symptomatic one.
**Diagnostic:** "Is this making us more capable or more dependent?"

---

## 3. Limits to Growth
**Structure:** Reinforcing loop drives growth. Growth encounters a limiting factor (balancing loop). Growth stops or reverses.

**Examples:**
- Company grows, can't hire fast enough (talent limit)
- Sales grow, market saturates
- Startup grows, processes/culture can't scale

**Leverage:** Don't push harder on growth. Address the limiting factor. (Goldratt: find the bottleneck.)
**Diagnostic:** "What constraint is slowing this? Are we pushing harder or addressing the limit?"

---

## 4. Tragedy of the Commons
**Structure:** Multiple agents share a resource. Each benefits from more use. Cost of overuse is distributed. Each rationally overuses. Resource collapses.

**Examples:**
- Overfishing, overgrazing, aquifer depletion
- Traffic congestion, antibiotic resistance
- Shared office budgets, meeting rooms

**Leverage:** Regulate access, privatise costs, make degradation visible, community self-governance (Ostrom).

---

## 5. Success to the Successful
**Structure:** Initial advantage → more resources → more success → more resources. Disadvantaged spiral down.

**Examples:**
- Wealth inequality (compound returns)
- Winner-take-all markets
- Popular products getting more shelf space

**Leverage:** Level the playing field periodically. Diversify the "games." Ensure losers stay viable.

---

## 6. Escalation
**Structure:** Two parties perceive each other as threats. Each responds by escalating. Reinforcing loop drives both to extremes.

**Examples:**
- Arms races, price wars, advertising wars
- Hostile negotiations, social media arguments, retaliatory tariffs

**Leverage:** Unilateral de-escalation, agreed limits, third-party mediation, reframe to positive-sum.

---

## 7. Growth and Underinvestment
**Structure:** Growth hits capacity limit. Investment delayed. Performance degrades. Expectations lower. Goals erode. System settles sub-optimally. Combines Limits to Growth + Eroding Goals.

**Examples:**
- Company grows but doesn't invest in infrastructure → "that's just how it is at scale"
- City grows but doesn't invest in transit → "that's city life"

**Leverage:** Maintain absolute performance standards. Invest proactively before the limit bites.

---

## 8. Eroding Goals (Drift to Low Performance)
**Structure:** Gap between goal and performance. Instead of raising performance, goal is lowered. Standards erode gradually.

**Examples:**
- Accepting longer customer service response times
- Personal fitness standards declining year over year
- Companies accepting higher defect rates

**Leverage:** Anchor goals to absolute standards or external benchmarks. Make erosion visible.
**Diagnostic:** "Have our standards slipped? What would the original standard look like?"

---

## 9. Accidental Adversaries
**Structure:** Allies inadvertently harm each other. Each perceives harm as intentional. Each retaliates. Mutual harm replaces mutual benefit.

**Examples:**
- Sales and engineering (overpromising ↔ building wrong things)
- Manager and team (micromanagement ↔ underperformance)
- Supplier and customer (price squeezing ↔ quality cutting)

**Leverage:** Make unintended harm visible. Direct communication. Realign incentives.

---

## 10. Policy Resistance
**Structure:** Multiple actors pulling the system toward different goals. Locked in place. Enormous effort, nothing changes.

**Example:** War on drugs — enforcement → prices rise → profit incentive → more suppliers → more enforcement.

**Leverage:** Find a shared goal. Realign actors' goals. Stop pulling against each other.

---

## Quick Recognition Checklist

| If you see... | Check for... |
|---|---|
| Quick fix that keeps repeating | Fixes That Fail / Shifting the Burden |
| Growing dependency on external solution | Shifting the Burden |
| Growth slowing | Limits to Growth |
| Standards declining over time | Eroding Goals |
| Two parties escalating | Escalation |
| Shared resource degrading | Tragedy of the Commons |
| Wealth/success concentrating | Success to the Successful |
| Allies becoming adversarial | Accidental Adversaries |
| Growth + deteriorating quality | Growth and Underinvestment |
| Everyone working hard, nothing changing | Policy Resistance |
